---------------------------------------------------------------
PART 1: WHAT I DID, START TO FINISH
---------------------------------------------------------------

1. Received your data (400 children, 34 columns) and the data
   dictionary, and set up a shared, version-controlled project so all
   the code and steps are tracked and fully reproducible.

2. Explored the data in detail — every variable: its distribution,
   how much was missing, and any impossible/odd values.

3. Sorted all variables by the role they play in the analysis:
   - the cognitive outcomes (the KABC ability scores),
   - the pollution exposures (PM2.5 prenatal, age 1, age 4),
   - background factors to adjust for (mother's age, education,
     ethnicity, marital status, child sex),
   - the "fairness" factor we want to test (mother's education / SES),
   - and variables that must be LEFT OUT because they happen as a
     RESULT of pollution (e.g. birthweight, child growth) — including
     these would hide the very effect we're trying to measure.

4. Cleaned the data and split it into three parts — a training set
   (to build the model), plus validation and test sets that I held
   back untouched to check the model honestly. I was careful that no
   information leaked between them.

5. Built the planned model (a Bayesian "Weighted Quantile Sum" burden
   index). In plain terms, it:
   - combines the three pollution time-points into ONE burden score
     per child, letting the data decide how much each time-point counts,
   - links that score to FOUR cognitive abilities at the same time,
   - adjusts for the background factors,
   - and tests whether pollution harms poorer children more.
   It also handles the large amount of missing education data properly,
   building that uncertainty into the results rather than guessing.

6. Ran the model, ran the full set of technical health checks, and
   tested its predictions on the held-back validation and test children.


---------------------------------------------------------------
PART 2: WHERE WE ARE NOW
---------------------------------------------------------------

THE GOOD NEWS (the method works):
- The statistical engine runs correctly and passed every technical
  reliability check. The model itself is sound and reusable.

THE HONEST NEWS (what the data can currently tell us):
- Right now the model can barely predict a child's cognitive scores
  from pollution + background factors — the signal is very weak.
- The one association that stands out is a SMALL link between higher
  early-life pollution and LOWER fluid reasoning (problem-solving
  ability). Everything else is inconclusive.
- We CANNOT yet say which life-stage (womb vs age 1 vs age 4) matters
  most, and we cannot yet answer the fairness question.

THE MAIN REASON — and this is the most important point:
- In the pollution data, one single exact value repeats for a very
  large share of children:
     * prenatal PM2.5 = 61.30 for about 38% of children
     * PM2.5 at age 1 = 54.80 for about 45% of children
     * PM2.5 at age 4 = 64.13 for about 18% of children
  This strongly suggests that missing pollution readings were filled
  in with a single average value. If so, that "filled-in" value carries
  no real information, which is exactly why the model can't tell which
  time-window matters. Until this is clarified, the burden index can't
  do the job the study is designed for.

So the next step depends mostly on your answers below — especially
about the pollution data.
